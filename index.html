Computer Science

Hello.

I am Oliwia, a first-year PhD student in Computer Science at the University of Maryland. My research, guided by my advisors Leo Lampropoulos and Ian Miers, explores Programming Languages and Cryptography, with a focus on using formal methods and zero-knowledge proofs to verify private systems without exposing sensitive details.

As an undergrad, I worked with Marco Morazán and my research partner Tijana Minić on Automata Theory, extending the domain-specific language FSM with tools to better understand nondeterminism.

I aim for my work to be driven by real-world application.

Interests

I used to be a banker but then I lost interest

  • Cybersecurity

    In Cybersecurity, I am interested in making systems both private and secure. My work looks at combining cryptographic tools like Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs) with formal methods to verify system properties, even in hostile environments. I am especially drawn to scenarios where privacy and security intersect, such as ensuring fair and private financial competition.

  • Cryptography

    I am interested in Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs) and how they enable verifying computations without revealing private data. During my graduate degree, I started learning about their applications in privacy-preserving systems. My goal is to use ZKPs for proving things like the correctness of trading algorithms while keeping sensitive details hidden.

  • Programming Languages

    My interest in Programming Languages started with Automata Theory during my undergrad, where I worked on extending FSM, a domain-specific language for state machines. My research team and I developed tools like computation graphs to explain nondeterminism visually. Now, I am exploring how to bring formal verification into my graduate research, focusing on proving properties of systems like financial or cryptographic algorithms.

  • Finance

    With a background in Mathematical Finance and a passion for Cryptocurrencies, I want to bridge finance with tech. I am exploring how to prove properties about trading algorithms, like fairness or integrity, without exposing their inner black magic. This combines my interests in Finance, Programming Languages, and Cryptography into one cohesive research direction.
